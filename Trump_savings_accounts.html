What Is a Trump Account?

Trump Accounts are a new type of tax-advantaged savings account for children, created under the "Working Families Tax Cuts" provisions of the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed into law by President Trump on July 4, 2025. They are the first federal child savings accounts in U.S. history.

$1,000
One-time federal contribution for each eligible child born 2025–2028
$5,000/yr
Combined annual limit on individual + employer contributions (indexed for inflation after 2027)
Age 18
Account converts to a standard traditional IRA on Jan. 1 of the year the child turns 18
Legally speaking: a Trump Account is a traditional IRA held for a minor. All accounts are initially administered by BNY (in partnership with Robinhood), though they can later be rolled over to a Trump Account at another financial institution. Each child may have only one funded Trump Account.

The $1,000 Federal Pilot Contribution

The federal government makes a one-time $1,000 deposit into the Trump Account of every eligible child. Use the checker below to see if a child qualifies.

Requirements

Who Gets the $1,000?

  • Born between January 1, 2025 and December 31, 2028
  • A U.S. citizen
  • Has a valid Social Security number
  • No prior pilot-program election made and processed on their behalf

The election is filed — typically by a parent or guardian who expects the child to be their "qualifying child" for tax purposes that year — using Form 4547, Trump Account Election(s).

The $1,000 can be claimed any time during the growth period (birth through Dec. 31 of the year the child turns 17) — no rush after birth. It does not count against the $5,000 annual contribution limit.

Opening an Account

Who's Eligible

The Child

Any child who has not turned 18 before the end of the calendar year of the election and who has a valid Social Security number. (The $1,000 pilot deposit has its own, narrower birth-year rules above.)

Who Can Open It

Priority Order

  • 1. Legal guardian
  • 2. Parent
  • 3. Adult sibling
  • 4. Grandparent

The IRS has requested comment on extending eligibility — including for foster children, orphans, emancipated minors, and wards of the state. In June 2026, First Lady Melania Trump announced the "Fostering the Future" program, letting state child-welfare agencies open accounts for foster youth in their care.

How

5–10 Minutes

Sign in to an IRS account (via ID.me) and submit Form 4547, or apply through the TrumpAccounts.gov portal. You'll need the child's Social Security number, date of birth, and address.

Who Can Contribute?

Contributions could not be made before July 4, 2026 — accounts opened for contributions on that date. Three channels feed a Trump Account:

Up to $5,000 / year combined

Individual Contributions

  • Parents, family members, and other individuals may contribute after-tax cash.
  • Combined annual limit of $5,000 per child (indexed for inflation starting after 2027).
  • Contributions are not tax-deductible.
  • The federal $1,000 pilot deposit does not count against this cap.
Up to $2,500 / year

Employer Contributions

  • Employers may contribute up to $2,500 per year to the account of a minor employee or of an adult employee's dependent.
  • Counts toward the overall $5,000 annual cap.
  • Excluded from the employee's taxable income.
  • Deductible by the employer as a compensation expense.
No dollar limit

"Qualified General" Contributions

  • Governments and charities may contribute through Treasury without a dollar limit.
  • Must be distributed in equal amounts to every beneficiary in a defined class (e.g., everyone in a state, region, or birth year).
  • Not taxable income to the beneficiary; does not count against the $5,000 cap.

Notable pledges: the Michael & Susan Dell Foundation's $6.25 billion commitment ($250 to every child under age 11), an additional $250 from the Dalio Foundation for Connecticut children, and pledges from a number of private employers.

Investments, Withdrawals & Taxes

From account opening until January 1 of the year the beneficiary turns 18 (the "growth period"), funds must be invested in mutual funds or ETFs that:

  • Track a broad index of primarily U.S. equities (such as the S&P 500);
  • Avoid leverage; and
  • Charge fees no higher than 0.1%.

Money generally cannot be withdrawn under any circumstances during the growth period, except upon the death of the account beneficiary.

One exception: in the calendar year the beneficiary turns 17, the account's assets may be transferred to an ABLE account (a tax-advantaged savings account for people with disabilities).

Starting January 1 of the year the beneficiary turns 18, the Trump Account converts into a standard traditional IRA subject to ordinary IRA distribution rules.

Withdrawals before age 59½ generally trigger a 10% early-withdrawal penalty unless an exception applies — e.g., first-time home purchase, qualified education expenses, or disaster recovery — each with its own dollar limits.

  • Contributions are made with after-tax dollars and are not taxed again on withdrawal.
  • Investment earnings compound tax-deferred inside the account.
  • Earnings are taxed as ordinary income (not capital-gains rates) when eventually withdrawn.
  • Because contributions from different sources (individual, employer, government/nonprofit) may be tracked separately, the tax treatment of a given withdrawal can be complex.

How They Compare

OptionStrengthsTrade-offs
Trump Account Free $1,000 federal deposit (if eligible); tax-deferred growth; low-fee index investing built in; anyone can contribute. Locked until 18; earnings taxed as ordinary income; early-withdrawal penalties after 18 unless an exception applies.
529 Plan More tax-advantageous for education-specific savings; up to $35,000 of unused funds can be rolled into a Roth IRA. Best suited to education expenses.
Taxable Brokerage Gains typically taxed at lower long-term capital-gains rates; fully flexible access. No tax deferral — yet can be competitive with (or superior to) a Trump Account in some scenarios.
What commentators say: most families should generally prioritize college savings and their own retirement accounts before maximizing Trump Account contributions.
